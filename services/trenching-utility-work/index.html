We Dig Where Others Are Afraid To

Trenching is the kind of work where a mistake is expensive or dangerous. Hit a buried line and you have a hazard, a repair bill, and a stopped job. We treat what is already in the ground as the first thing to deal with, not an afterthought.

  • We call it in first through 811 so buried utilities are marked before a blade ever goes in the ground.
  • We dig to the right depth for the line or footer going in, so it sits where code and the job call for.
  • We keep the runs clean and accurate so what gets laid in the trench connects and drains the way it should.
  • We work around what is buried so existing lines come through the job intact.

How We Handle Every Trenching Job

Good trenching is careful work, not just fast digging. Here is what goes into it.

1

Locate and Mark

Calling in 811 and confirming what is buried before any digging starts.

2

Plan the Run

Setting the route, depth, and grade for the line, footer, or foundation going in.

3

Trench and Excavate

Digging clean, accurate trenches to the depth and line the job needs.

4

Backfill and Restore

Filling the trench back in and leaving the ground compacted and tidy once the line is set.

Why Safe, Code-Compliant Trenching Matters

Trenching done wrong is one of the costlier mistakes on a job site, and one of the more dangerous. Doing it right keeps the project moving and everyone safe.

  • Skipping the locate risks cutting a live electric, gas, or water line, which is a hazard and a repair you pay for.
  • A trench at the wrong depth leaves a line that fails inspection or sits where it should not, meaning you dig twice.
  • A sloppy run leaves lines that do not connect or drain right and a backfill that settles unevenly.
  • Trenching done right passes inspection, protects what is already buried, and keeps your build on schedule.

Common Questions About Trenching and Utility Work

Straight answers to the questions we hear most from homeowners, builders, and property owners who need lines or footers put in the ground.

How deep does a trench need to be for utility lines?

Trench depth depends on what is going in it. Electric, water, and septic lines each have their own depth requirements set by code and by the utility, and footers and foundations are dug to the lines and depth the build calls for. Going too shallow leaves a line that can fail inspection or get damaged later, so depth is something we dig to spec, not by guess. We handle utility, septic, and foundation trenching across Skiatook and northeast Oklahoma dug to the right depth for the job.

Do you call 811 before digging?

Yes, every time. Calling 811 gets buried utilities located and marked before any trench is dug, which keeps the crew safe and keeps you from cutting a live line. It is the first step on any trenching job we take, not an optional one. Skipping it is how people hit electric, gas, and water lines, and we do not skip it.

Can you trench for a septic system?

We handle the trenching and excavation for septic lines, dug to the depth and grade the system needs to drain right. Whether we handle the full septic scope depends on the job, so give us a call with the details and we will tell you exactly what we can do for your property across Osage, Tulsa, and Rogers County.

What is augering used for?

Augering bores clean, accurate holes in the ground, usually for posts, footings, and similar work where you need a precise hole at depth. It is faster and cleaner than digging by hand and puts the hole exactly where you need it. We offer augering alongside our trenching work throughout northeast Oklahoma.

What areas do you serve for trenching work?
