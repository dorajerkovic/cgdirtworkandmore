A Pond Is Only as Good as the Ground Under It

A hole is not a pond. Whether it holds water comes down to the soil, where the runoff feeds it, and where the water wants to escape. We studied how soil and water actually behave, and that knowledge goes into every pond we dig.

Here is what that means for your pond:

  • We read the soil first to know whether the ground will hold water or let it seep away before you ever pay for a dig.
  • We follow the water to see where your runoff comes from and how to feed the pond so it stays full.
  • We shape the banks right so they hold their line instead of caving and silting the pond back in.
  • We plan the drainage so the pond keeps the water you want and sheds the overflow where it does no harm.

How We Dig a Pond That Holds

Every pond comes down to getting four things right. Here is the work behind a pond that keeps water.

1

Site and Soil Assessment

Reading the ground, the runoff, and the drainage before we dig so the pond holds water once it is full.

2

Excavation and Shaping

Digging, shaping the banks, and setting the depth and slope so the pond works and lasts.

3

Holding the Water In

Building up the dam or berm and packing the ground where the pond needs it to keep its level through the dry months.

4

Overflow and Drainage

Setting the spillway and drainage so the pond keeps the water you want and sheds the overflow where it does no harm.

Why Having An Expert Build The Right Pond Matters

A pond that was dug wrong is a constant headache. Doing it right the first time means water when you need it and one less thing to fix.

  • A pond that will not hold drains your investment into the ground and leaves you hauling water or digging again.
  • A silted-in pond loses depth and capacity every year until it is more mud flat than water source.
  • Bad placement or shaping sends your runoff the wrong way and can flood ground you actually wanted to keep dry.
  • A pond done right holds its level, supports your livestock or your fishing, and works through the dry stretch of an Oklahoma summer.
